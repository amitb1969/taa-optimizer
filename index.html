TAA Optimizer — How It Works

What This Tool Does

This is a Tactical Asset Allocation (TAA) optimizer for an absolute return portfolio of ETFs. Given your current holdings, conviction scores, and risk/exposure constraints, it computes optimal target weights that maximize risk-adjusted conviction while respecting every limit you set.

The Objective Function

The optimizer solves:

max   Σ wi αi  −  (λ/2) wT Σ w  −  κ Σ|wi − wi0|

In plain English: maximize your conviction-weighted exposure, minus a penalty for portfolio risk, minus a penalty for trading away from current weights.

wiWeight of ETF i in the portfolio (sums to 100%)
αiYour weekly alpha score (0, 1, 2, or 3) based on technicals. 3 = strongest conviction
Σ (Sigma)The covariance matrix capturing how all ETFs move together. Built from correlations x individual volatilities
wTΣwPortfolio variance, a single number capturing total portfolio risk including all cross-correlations
λ (Alpha Weight)Controls how much alpha scores influence weights vs pure diversification. 0 = pure min-variance. 10 = concentrate in high-score names
wi0Current weight of ETF i (before rebalancing), the reference point for turnover
κ (T-Cost)Transaction cost penalty. Penalizes every % of weight moved away from current holdings. 0 = free to trade. 10 = heavy penalty, optimizer stays close to current weights

What the Optimizer Considers

The optimizer finds weights subject to these constraints simultaneously:

Position boundsMin and Max % per ETF (default 0-30%). Set in the Optimizer tab.
Group limitsMax/Min % per Asset Class, Geography, Theme, and Risk Class. Set in Constraints tab.
NO SELL / NO BUYLocks on individual ETFs. NO SELL = optimized weight cannot go below current. NO BUY = optimized weight cannot go above current. Click the Lock column to cycle.
Weights sum to 100%Fully invested constraint. All weights must sum to exactly 100%.

The Solver (Dual-Ascent QP)

The optimizer runs in two phases:

Phase 1 — Dual Ascent: Starts with a diagonal approximation of the covariance matrix (ignoring cross-correlations). Uses Lagrange multipliers for each group constraint. Iteratively adjusts these — if a group is over its cap, the penalty increases, pushing weights down. Converges in 50-200 iterations.

Phase 2 — Gradient Projection Polish: Takes the Phase 1 solution and refines it using the full covariance matrix (all cross-correlations). Uses projected gradient ascent with subgradients for the L1 turnover penalty. Runs 2,000 iterations for precision.

Typical solve time: 30-80ms for 17 assets with 15+ group constraints.

Transaction Cost Penalty (T-Cost Slider)

The T-Cost slider controls κ — the penalty the optimizer pays for moving weight away from current holdings. Every percentage point of turnover costs κ in objective value. The optimizer will only trade if the alpha or risk-reduction benefit exceeds this cost.

0 (off)No penalty. Full freedom. Can produce 60-80% turnover. Use for initial setup or major regime changes.
2 (default)Moderate penalty. Trades only when conviction or risk benefit justifies cost. Typical turnover 25-40%.
5 (firm)Strong penalty. Only significant alpha changes or clear risk reduction triggers trades. Turnover 10-20%.
10 (max)Very heavy penalty. Portfolio barely moves. Only extreme situations generate trades.

How it works: The term adds an L1 penalty around current weights. At w = w0 (no trade), the cost is zero. Each unit of weight moved costs κ. Same structure as a Lasso penalty — it drives small, marginal trades to exactly zero, producing sparse trade lists.

Practical guidance: Start with T-Cost = 2. If turnover is too high, increase to 3-4. For monthly rebalancing, 2-3 is typical. For weekly rebalancing, use 4-6. Set to 0 only for initial portfolio construction or major thesis shifts.

Correlation Stress Slider

This slider controls how much you assume markets will move together during a crisis.

In normal times, different countries and asset classes don’t move in lockstep — Greece might be up while Japan is down, gold might zig while oil zags. That’s diversification, and it’s why you hold multiple positions.

But in a crisis (think March 2020, 2008), everything drops together. Correlations spike — assets that were independent suddenly become correlated. Your “diversified” portfolio suddenly acts like one big position.

The slider simulates this:

0 — CALMCorrelations are lower than normal. You’re telling the optimizer “diversification will work even better than usual.” It spreads weight across more positions because it believes they’ll offset each other.
50 — NORMALUses correlations as-is from historical data. Default starting point.
100 — CRISISCorrelations are higher than normal. Positive correlations get pushed up ~20% (e.g. SPY–EWP goes from 0.70 → 0.84). You’re telling the optimizer “assume everything will sell off together.” It responds by putting more weight in truly uncorrelated assets (SHY, GLD) and less in positions that look diversified in calm markets but won’t be in a crash.

Practical guidance: Keep at 50 for normal weekly rebalancing. If you’re worried about a macro shock (tariff escalation, geopolitical event, credit stress), push to 70–80 before optimizing — the output will be a more defensive portfolio. You don’t need to touch it every week; it’s a scenario tool for when your risk radar is flashing.

Key Metrics (Analytics Tab)

Portfolio VolSquare root of portfolio variance. Total portfolio volatility from the covariance matrix.
Diversification RatioSum of weighted individual vols divided by portfolio vol. Higher = correlations reducing risk more. 1.0x = no benefit.
Risk ContributionEach ETF marginal contribution to portfolio risk. An ETF can have 5% weight but 15% risk contribution if highly correlated with the rest.

Country Model Integration

Tab 3 (Country Model) takes composite scores from your external country model and converts them to ETF alpha signals:

InputCountry composite scores (0-100) from your model, with Value, Momentum, Liquidity sub-scores
Z-ScoreEach country score is standardized: (score - mean) / std across the universe
MappingCountries are mapped to ETFs. Multi-country ETFs (EEM, EFA) use the average Z-score of their constituent countries
CutoffsZ > 1.0 = alpha 3, Z > 0.3 = alpha 2, Z > -0.3 = alpha 1, below = alpha 0
ApplyClick "Apply to Alpha" to push scores into the optimizer. Non-mapped ETFs (bonds, commodities, cash) keep manual alpha.

Weekly Workflow

1.Update holdings — paste from broker CSV or type dollar amounts in Tab 1
2.Import country model — upload CSV in Tab 3, click Apply to Alpha
3.Set manual alphas — for non-country ETFs (bonds, gold, cash) in Tab 4
4.Review constraints — check theme tags in Tab 2 and limits in Tab 7
5.Set locks — NO SELL / NO BUY on individual positions in Tab 4
6.Run optimizer — click OPTIMIZE. Review trade column.
7.Review and send — Tab 5 (Analytics) for risk, Tab 6 (Port Changes) for the rebalance memo to send to team
